## TL;DR

Using a mitochondrial marker (12S/16S or cytochrome b) together with multiple independent nuclear loci (IRBP, vWF, introns, UCEs or genome-wide SNPs) gives the best balance for delimitation and phylogeny; mitochondrial loci resolve recent splits but can mislead when introgression or sex-biased gene flow occurred.  

----

## Marker performance and combinations

Mitochondrial and nuclear markers each contribute different information, so effective primate studies combine them and increase nuclear sampling for robustness. Empirical primate studies routinely pair mtDNA (12S/16S, cyt b, or complete mitogenomes) with nuclear loci such as IRBP and vWF, or with many independent nuclear introns/UCEs or SNPs to resolve both shallow and deeper nodes [1] [2] [3] [4].

- **Mitochondrial markers** 12S, 16S, cyt b: frequently used for recent divergences and for obtaining complete mitochondrial genomes from degraded or noninvasive samples; they give high resolution for matrilineal history and are practical for species discovery pipelines [1] [5].  
- **Nuclear protein-coding loci** IRBP and vWF: provide biparental signal and help test mtDNA inferences; these loci have been informative across primate genera and are standard components of multilocus studies [1] [2].  
- **Nuclear introns and UCEs** many independent introns or UCE flanking regions increase power to estimate species trees, accommodate incomplete lineage sorting, and recover rapid radiations when sampled broadly [6] [4] [3].  
- **Genome-wide SNPs / reduced-representation data** (RAD/ddRAD/target capture): enable dense sampling across the genome and are effective for delimitation and detecting admixture, with different protocols showing differing temporal informativeness [7] [8].

## Strengths and limitations

Choosing markers requires weighing speed of evolution, inheritance mode, and susceptibility to non-vertical processes; combining marker types mitigates individual limitations. Multiple primate case studies show mitochondrial and nuclear data can be concordant for many nodes but discordant where hybridization, sex-biased gene flow, or strong incomplete lineage sorting occur [9] [10] [11] [3].

- **Strengths of mtDNA**  
  - **High resolution for recent splits** useful in delimitation and when sample quality limits nuclear recovery [5] [3].  
  - **Ease of recovery** from museum and fecal samples supports broad taxon sampling [5].  
- **Limitations of mtDNA**  
  - **Maternal inheritance bias** can misrepresent species history after introgression or male‑mediated gene flow, producing discordant topologies relative to nuclear markers [9] [10] [12].  
  - **Single-locus stochasticity** makes mtDNA vulnerable to lineage sorting and selective sweeps; it should not be the sole evidence for species delimitation [2] [3].  
- **Strengths of nuclear loci (IRBP, vWF, introns, UCEs, SNPs)**  
  - **Biparental signal and multiple independent histories** reduce the risk of single-locus artifacts and better capture population-level processes [6] [4] [1].  
  - **Power to detect ILS and admixture** when many loci are sampled or genomic data are used [11] [13].  
- **Limitations of nuclear loci**  
  - **Lower per-locus variability** than mtDNA, so multiple independent loci (or many sites from sequence-capture/genomic approaches) are required to achieve resolution [6] [7].  
  - **Method sensitivity**: concatenation can give strongly supported but conflicting topologies under rapid radiations or strong gene-tree heterogeneity if gene-tree heterogeneity is not modeled [7].

## Best practices for multilocus phylogenetic studies

Design studies to combine marker types, sample many independent nuclear loci, and use coalescent-aware inference and admixture testing; several primate studies exemplify these practices. Coalescent delimitation and species-tree methods, explicit tests for hybridization, and comparison among methods help distinguish incomplete lineage sorting from introgression [14] [15] [10] [11].

- **Assemble complementary datasets**  
  - **mtDNA plus nuclear loci** Combine 12S/16S or cyt b (or mitogenomes) with several nuclear genes (IRBP, vWF) and additional introns/UCEs or SNPs to span timescales and inheritance modes [1] [2] [4].  
- **Scale nuclear sampling to the question**  
  - **Multiple independent loci** Use many introns/UCEs or genome‑scale SNPs rather than relying on one or two nuclear loci, because nuclear markers are less variable per locus but collectively informative [6] [4] [7].  
- **Use coalescent and network approaches**  
  - **Species-tree and delimitation methods** Apply multispecies coalescent delimitation (e.g., BPP-style or Bayes-factor approaches) alongside exploratory methods to provide objective species hypotheses [14] [15] [16].  
  - **Test for introgression/ILS** Where mt/nuclear conflict appears, use explicit tests or phylogenetic networks to assess hybridization versus incomplete lineage sorting [10] [13] [11].  
- **Compare inference methods and sensitivity**  
  - **Concatenation versus coalescent** Run both approaches and evaluate conflicts; mismodeling gene-tree heterogeneity can produce strongly supported but incorrect results in rapid radiations [7] [3].  
- **Match markers to temporal scale**  
  - **Choose markers by phylogenetic informativeness** Markers differ in temporal signal (mtDNA often better for recent splits; some nuclear datasets better for deep nodes), so choose and interpret loci accordingly [7] [4].  
- **Include sex-linked loci when relevant**  
  - **Detect sex-biased processes** Add Y‑chromosome or X-linked data if male- or female-mediated gene flow is suspected, because mtDNA alone cannot detect such biases [12] [17].  
- **Prioritize sampling and data quality**  
  - **Broad taxon and geographic sampling** and, where possible, noninvasive or museum-derived mitogenomes can expand coverage rapidly but must be paired with nuclear data to avoid mtDNA-only artifacts [5] [18].
