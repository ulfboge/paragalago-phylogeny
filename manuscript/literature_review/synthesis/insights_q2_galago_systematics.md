## TL;DR

Molecular and acoustic work shows the East African dwarf galagos include multiple cryptic species, with Paragalago (zanzibaricus/cocos complex) validated by multilocus and mitochondrial data and Galagoides revealed as polyphyletic with distinct east and central‑west clades. Major debates concern generic boundaries, unresolved named forms (e.g., orinus, Taita populations), and the need for denser multilocus/genomic sampling.

----

## Molecular phylogenetic findings

The literature combines single‑marker mitochondrial studies, multilocus nuclear datasets, and coalescent species‑tree approaches to clarify relationships among dwarf galagos and to detect deep cryptic lineages. 

- **Large multilocus phylogeny** — A 27‑locus phylogeny recovered Euoticus as sister to other galagids and found that Galagoides (as traditionally circumscribed) is not monophyletic, implying the Zanzibar/coastal complex requires taxonomic revision [1].  
- **Supermatrix and biogeography** — A broader supermatrix (53 nuclear loci + 3 mitochondrial markers) corroborated that the dwarf galagos labelled Galagoides are polyphyletic and fall into two well‑defined clades (an eastern clade and a central‑west African clade), with most genus‑level diversifications already present by the Late Miocene [2].  
- **Paragalago complex studies** — Focused genetic work on the zanzibaricus complex combined mitochondrial cytochrome b sampling across populations (50 samples) and multilocus species‑delimitation, finding separate mitochondrial clades and supporting at least three distinct lineages within the complex, consistent with multilocus species delimitation [3] [4].  
- **mtDNA signals and earlier work** — Earlier mitochondrial gene surveys also showed that some taxa placed in Galagoides are not closely allied and that mt markers (e.g., 16S, cytochrome b) can give differing resolution among clades [5].  
- **Vocal versus genetic patterns** — Playback and acoustic analyses found distinct, species‑specific loud calls within the Paragalago zanzibaricus complex, but vocal similarity does not always mirror molecular sister‑group relationships and sensory‑drive processes likely shaped call divergence in different habitats [6].

## Taxonomic controversies and unresolved names

Taxonomic status and generic limits remain the core disputes, with several named forms poorly settled by current data. 

- **Generic circumscription** — Multiple molecular studies indicate Galagoides (sensu lato) is polyphyletic and that the Zanzibar/coastal lineage should likely be placed in a different genus (the literature explicitly calls for a new generic name for the Zanzibar complex) [1] [2].  
- **Validity and naming of coastal forms** — Reviews and taxonomic treatments argue that the Kenya coast form should be called Galagoides cocos (now treated in many genetic/acoustic studies as Paragalago cocos by recent authors), and vocal and penile characters support its separation from G. zanzibaricus and G. granti [7] [8] [9] [10].  
- **Eastern Arc and montane forms** — The Mountain/Taita and Eastern Arc populations (e.g., G. orinus, Udzungwa forms, Taita dwarf) show morphological and vocal distinctiveness but remain incompletely sampled genetically; some populations may represent undescribed species or subspecies and require DNA confirmation [11] [12] [13] [14].  
- **Character conflict and locus discordance** — Mitochondrial versus nuclear markers and acoustic characters sometimes give conflicting signals (e.g., calls not strictly congruent with gene trees), leaving species limits ambiguous in contact zones and recent radiations [6] [5] [1].  
- **Morphology versus molecules** — Penile morphology and craniodental metrics provide useful taxonomic signals for several eastern taxa (supporting distinctness of G. cocos, G. granti, G. zanzibaricus, and separation of G. orinus and G. rondoensis), but morphometrics alone cannot resolve all taxa and sometimes groups museum skulls inconsistently with molecular results [9] [10] [15].

## Lines of evidence from acoustics and morphology

Acoustic signals and reproductive morphology have been central to delimiting cryptic dwarf galagos and often guide field identifications where molecular data are lacking.

- **Acoustic discrimination** — Loud advertising calls reliably differentiate coastal/eastern dwarf galagos in the field and matched genetic clades at high assignment rates in combined acoustic/genetic studies, with ∼90% correct acoustic assignment between Paragalago cocos and P. zanzibaricus in one mitochondrial study [3] [7].  
- **Playback and sensory drive** — Playback experiments and soundscape analyses indicate song structure differences among the zanzibaricus complex, and sensory‑drive processes (habitat acoustics and ear morphology) likely contributed to call evolution and reproductive isolation among cryptic taxa [6].  
- **Penile morphology** — Comparative penile anatomy distinguishes several eastern species: G. cocos, G. granti, and G. zanzibaricus show similar spine distributions but differ in glans shape, whereas G. orinus and G. rondoensis exhibit divergent penile morphologies consistent with distinct phylogenetic positions [9] [10].  
- **Craniodental morphometrics** — Skull measurements separate some species reliably (e.g., demidoff vs thomasi) but have limited power to resolve all dwarf galagos and can conflict with molecular placements for some type specimens [15].

## Remaining gaps and recommended next steps

Several concrete unresolved issues persist and point to priorities for future work to stabilize systematics and taxonomy.

- **Genus‑level revision** — A formal taxonomic revision is needed to split Galagoides sensu lato into monophyletic genera (especially to rename the Zanzibar/coastal complex) based on genome‑scale data and comprehensive type‑series sampling [1] [2].  
- **Broader genomic sampling** — Dense geographic sampling using multilocus coalescent or phylogenomic SNP datasets is required to resolve recent divergences, gene‑tree discordance, and possible hybrid zones among Paragalago/Galagoides taxa [1] [4] [16].  
- **Integrative delimitation in contact zones** — Integrative studies that combine acoustics, morphology (including penile characters), mtDNA, nuclear loci, and genomic SNPs are necessary where parapatry or narrow sympatry occurs (e.g., along the Rufiji and in Eastern Arc/Mafia/Zanzibar systems) to delimit species and detect gene flow [7] [6] [9] [10].  
- **Undersampled populations** — Targeted genetic work on Taita Hills and other isolated Eastern Arc populations (noted as calling like P. cocos but unsampled genetically) is an urgent priority for taxonomic clarification and conservation planning [14] [13].  
- **Methodological advances** — New, faster multispecies coalescent delimitation tools have already revealed inconsistencies with older taxonomies in Galagidae and should be applied to large genomic datasets for robust, reproducible species hypotheses [16].

Insufficient evidence

- Several specific taxonomic assignments (for example formal generic names to replace Galagoides for the Zanzibar complex, and the definitive species identity of some montane/Taita populations) remain unresolved in the supplied literature and require new genomic and integrative analyses; therefore, detailed resolutions of those points are currently unsupported by the available papers.
