## TL;DR

Pleistocene and older climate cycles plus topographic barriers produced repeated isolation and intermittent connectivity across East African montane, Eastern Arc and coastal forests, generating both ancient paleoendemic lineages and recent neoendemics. Refugia, rivers and low-elevation stepping-stones together dictated lineage splits, demographic shifts and idiosyncratic speciation patterns.

----

## Phylogeographic patterns

East African montane, Eastern Arc and coastal forest taxa show strong geographic structuring tied to mountain blocks, rifts and rivers, with both deep and shallow divergences across groups. Patterns include clear north–south and between-massif breaks, localized endemism on specific sky islands, and congruent barriers for multiple taxa.

- **Mountain breaks** Evidence for consistent phylogeographic breaks across mountain gaps such as the Usambara–Nguru/Nguu divide occurs in multiple bird lineages, with 10 of 14 showing breaks across that 125 km lowland barrier [1].  
- **Rift and depression barriers** Reciprocal monophyly and high exclusivity on either side of the Great Rift System are reported in plants, indicating restricted gene flow and long-term isolation [2].  
- **Riverine and coastal structure** River channels and hydrological barriers strongly structure coastal and generalist amphibians, producing congruent intra‑specific population structure in several taxa [3].  
- **Regional contrasts** Albertine Rift and Itombwe populations tend to be older and more persistently isolated than Kenyan Highlands populations, which often show lower diversity consistent with smaller historical population sizes [4].  
- **Cradle areas** The Ethiopian Highlands act as a source or cradle for several Afromontane radiations, with subsequent southward dispersal into the broader Afromontane archipelago [5].

## Biogeographic history

Historical timelines combine deep (Miocene–Oligocene) persistence for some clades with Plio‑Pleistocene fragmentation and recent radiations in others, shaped by climate cycles, rifting and orographic complexity. Below is a concise comparison across three focal regional systems.

| Region | Dominant timing of splits | Mechanisms shaping history | Representative barriers or corridors |
|---|---:|---|---|
| Eastern Arc Mountains | Mix of ancient lineages and Pleistocene splits (some clades pre‑Pliocene; others Plio‑Pleistocene) | Long-term persistence in stable sky islands plus episodic vicariance and stepping‑stone dispersal via lower hills [1] [6] [7] | Usambara–Nguru gaps, riparian corridors, Southern Volcanic Highlands absence [1] [8] |
| Coastal forests | Mostly late Pliocene–Pleistocene divergences with idiosyncratic responses | Forest refugia, riverine barriers, and ecological shifts producing both persistence and habitat switching [3] [9] | River systems, hydrological/topographic divides, coastal microrefugia [3] [9] |
| Wider Afromontane archipelago | Late Miocene origins with Pleistocene dispersal/contraction events | Cradle in Ethiopian Highlands, dispersal across depressions during favorable periods, vicariance from rifting and climatic drying [5] [10] [11] | Turkana Depression, Great Rift, past lowland corridors during glacial intervals [5] [10] [11] |

- **Ancient versus recent** Several lineages (e.g., some brevicipitid frogs and forest chameleons) trace persistence back to Oligocene–Miocene times and reflect paleoendemics of stable refugia [7] [6].  
- **Plio‑Pleistocene influence** A wave of more recent diversification and range fragmentation, often during Pleistocene climatic cycles, produced many neoendemic montane radiations and population partitions [1] [12] [8].  
- **Role of geology and corridors** Geological features (Great Rift, Turkana Depression) repeatedly acted as barriers, whereas modeled LGM climates and paleocorridors permitted intermittent lowland connectivity between montane regions [11] [10].

## Pleistocene refugia dynamics

Data from genetic phylogenies, demographic analyses and habitat modeling collectively indicate that Pleistocene refugia existed but their locations, persistence and roles varied among taxa and regions. Refugia acted both as long‑term museums preserving ancient lineages and as sources for recent radiations.

- **Modeling and ENMs** Paleoclimate and species distribution models retrodict persistence of montane forest on several Eastern Arc islands during climate cycles and suggest lowland corridors at glacial maxima linking many Afromontane regions [1] [11] [9].  
- **Genetic signatures of refugia** Deep haplotype divergence, nested range patterns in land snails, and localized exclusive alleles are consistent with contraction into refugia followed by post‑glacial expansions for many taxa [13] [14].  
- **Temporal heterogeneity** Divergence timing varies: some taxa diverged sequentially in the mid‑to‑late Pleistocene (e.g., chameleons ~0.93–0.59 Ma), while other groups retain lineages predating Pliocene events, indicating refugia stability for some clades but dynamic change for others [8] [6].  
- **Idiosyncratic taxon responses** Comparative genomic work in coastal amphibians shows congruent spatial structure but differing demographic histories and diversification modes, so refugial effects are taxon‑specific rather than uniform [3].  
- **Physical refugia types** Both montane sky‑islands and lowland microrefugia (coastal and gallery forests) are supported as important for lineage survival and divergence [9] [14].

## Role in speciation and lineage structure

Refugia, intermittent corridors and topographic fragmentation generated multiple speciation modes, producing a mosaic of ancient relicts and recent radiations that together create the region's high endemism. Both vicariance and ecological divergence contributed to lineage splitting.

- **Museums and cradles** Stable refugia preserved paleoendemic lineages over tens of millions of years in some taxa, while climatic cycling produced pulses of recent montane speciation creating neoendemics concentrated on isolated peaks [6] [7] [1].  
- **Speciation mechanisms** Empirical studies document vicariance from repeated forest contraction, peripatric isolation at range edges, and ecological divergence after habitat transitions (montane ↔ alpine or forest ↔ grassland) as operative modes [15] [16] [17].  
- **Evidence across taxa** Montane birds and greenbuls show recent montane speciation within the Plio‑Pleistocene, rodents and shrews reveal north–south and rift-driven splits, and plants and trees show deep phylogeographic structure often reflecting older migration corridors or early splits [12] [4] [2] [10].  
- **Lineage structure outcomes** The result is high genetic exclusivity and mountain‑specific clades, variable genetic diversity among massifs (e.g., low diversity in Kenyan Highlands vs higher isolation in the Albertine Rift), and mixed patterns of deep and shallow coexisting lineages across landscapes [4] [18] [19].  
- **Refugia and speciation role** In sum, refugia functioned both as long‑term repositories of ancient diversity and as arenas for recent diversification, but their relative importance differs among clades and regions, producing the present patchwork of endemic and divergent lineages [6] [1] [3].
